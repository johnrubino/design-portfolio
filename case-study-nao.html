The $1.2M Problem: When Four Systems Became One Workflow

How redesigning account opening reduced errors by 30% and gave advisors back 6 hours per week

The Day We Mapped the Chaos

In October 2023, our team gathered around a whiteboard to map the New Account Opening (NAO) workflow. What started as a simple exercise became a revelation: advisors had four different entry points into the same process, each living in a different system.

By the time we finished mapping, the board looked like a conspiracy theorist's wall—loops, dead ends, and arrows going everywhere. One researcher looked at it and said: "No wonder our NIGO rate is through the roof."

The legacy NAO process wasn't just broken—it was costing BNY millions in operational inefficiency, advisor time, and client trust.

Project Summary

Dimension Details
The Cost of Chaos The legacy NAO process was fragmented across NetX360, a CRM, and e-sign tools. Advisors spent 6+ hours per account re-entering data, while 40% of submissions came back Not-In-Good-Order (NIGO), requiring rework.
The Opportunity Create a single, unified, guided NAO/Client Onboarding (COB) workflow within the modern Wove advisor platform that works for both advisors (who value speed) and home office teams (who value accuracy).
My Role Studio Designer — I led the end-to-end redesign of the unified workflow experience, from synthesis of legacy system flows to designing and validating 6 core feature sets. I owned the interaction design for the To-Do tracker, Pre-Flow setup, and Documents table, while collaborating with a Lead Designer on information architecture and a Content Designer on guidance copy. I facilitated validation sessions with 18 users across 3 firms.
Timeline 6 months (Discovery → Design → Validation)
Business Impact 35% reduction in onboarding time, 45% reduction in manual data re-entry, and 30% drop in NIGO rates (validated outcomes). Estimated $1.2M annual savings in operational costs for a mid-sized firm.

The Conflict: When the System Becomes the Org Chart

Before we even touched a wireframe, we had to understand the true cost of the status quo. The challenge wasn't that advisors didn't know how to open accounts—the system actively worked against them.

The Legacy Process: A Four-System Odyssey

The numbers told a brutal story:

  • 6.2 hours — Average time to complete a single account opening
  • 4 entry points — Different starting points depending on account type
  • 40% NIGO rate — Nearly half of all submissions came back with errors
  • 3.7 touches per account — Average number of times an account ping-ponged between advisor and operations
  • 47% of advisor time — Spent on data re-entry across systems

But the qualitative pain was even more visceral:

"It takes too many clicks to find where I need to go. And when I finally get there, I have to enter the same client info I already put in the CRM."

— Senior Advisor, 12 years at firm

"We spend more time chasing missing documents than actually reviewing accounts. The system doesn't tell us what's missing until we dig through everything."

— Operations Manager

The Real Problem: Poorly Formed Plans, Not Poor Execution

As I dug deeper into the data and watched advisors struggle through sessions, a pattern emerged: The problem wasn't that people were bad at opening accounts—it was that the system never helped them form a good plan in the first place.

  • Advisors didn't know what documents were required until they were 80% through the process
  • Operations teams couldn't see progress in real-time
  • The KYC section appeared randomly in the flow, causing abandonment
  • No one had a shared view of "what's done" vs. "what's left"

Our Goal

Create a guided workflow that helps advisors and operations teams form a better account opening plan—one that's clear, collaborative, and complete from the start.

🔍

Deep Discovery: Quantifying the Chaos

I began by mapping the current state across all four legacy systems. This visualization was our first "Aha!" moment—it confirmed the problem wasn't a single feature, but the entire fragmented user journey.

Triangulating the Truth

Rather than conducting expensive new research, I partnered with the Pershing Research League to synthesize existing insights from:

  • Qualtrics Feedback from 240+ advisors across 18 firms
  • Pershing Research on operational inefficiencies (2022-2023)
  • PershingX Research on desired integration and automation
  • Support ticket analysis — 3,200+ tickets related to NAO issues in 12 months

The Redesign: From Four Systems to One Intelligent Workflow

The new design focused on making the workflow feel like a single, intelligent application that guides you through a complex process, not a collection of disconnected forms.

The Rendezvous Framework

Borrowing from Simon Pan's approach at Uber, I reframed the problem: We weren't just redesigning screens—we were creating a Rendezvous system where advisors, operations teams, and clients could all see the same plan and work toward completion together.

Solution 1: Pre-Flow Setup (Solving Zero Redundancy)

The Why

47% of advisor time was spent re-entering the same data across systems. We needed to capture and validate core information before the formal workflow began—but without adding friction.

The Hypothesis: If we could validate key data upfront (client name, account type, tax status), we could pre-fill 70%+ of the downstream forms and reduce errors at submission.

Solution 2: Dynamic To-Do Tracker (Solving Total Visibility)

The Why

Advisors and operations teams had no shared view of progress. Status check calls consumed 1.2 hours per account.

The Solution: A persistent, role-aware To-Do tracker that shows what's done, what's in progress, and what's next—visible to everyone involved in the account opening.

Solution 3: Documents Table (Solving Form Orchestration)

The Why

The 40% NIGO rate was primarily driven by missing or incorrect documents. Users didn't know what was required until too late.

The Solution: A unified Documents Table that shows all required forms upfront, their status (missing, in progress, completed), and allows bulk actions like download, upload, and e-sign.

What I Killed: The Smart Suggestions Feature

Not every idea survived validation. One feature I was particularly excited about—and ultimately killed—was Smart Suggestions.

The Idea

Based on client demographics and account type, the system would proactively suggest additional forms or account features (e.g., "Clients like this often add beneficiary designations" or "Consider adding IRA rollover documentation").

Why I Thought It Would Work

  • It would reduce NIGO by surfacing commonly missed forms
  • It leveraged ML/data to provide personalized guidance
  • It positioned Wove as an intelligent, proactive platform

Why It Failed

Testing revealed three critical issues:

  1. Advisors felt micromanaged: "I know my clients better than the system does"
  2. Cognitive overload: The suggestions appeared at exactly the wrong time—when users were already focused on completing required tasks
  3. Low trust in recommendations: Users questioned the accuracy and worried about liability if they followed a bad suggestion

The Learning

Just because you can add intelligence doesn't mean you should. Sometimes the best design is the one that gets out of the user's way. I learned that trust in automation must be earned incrementally—we couldn't jump straight to proactive recommendations without first nailing the basics.

This failure informed a key principle: Be smart in the background, be simple in the foreground.

Validation & Outcomes

Following the design phase, I facilitated live prototype validation sessions with 18 users (12 advisors, 6 home office) across 3 firms (small, mid-size, enterprise). The sessions confirmed that the redesigned flow improved clarity, reduced friction, and established a shared mental model between teams.

Validation Methodology

  • 12 task-based scenarios covering the full NAO flow
  • Comparative testing: Legacy NetX360 vs. Wove redesign
  • Mixed methods: Time-on-task, error rates, SUS surveys, and qualitative feedback
  • Cross-role testing: Paired advisor + operations sessions to observe collaboration

The Impact (Even When Designs Don't Ship)

Although the redesigned NAO experience was validated positively across users, it was not implemented due to shifting business priorities and platform dependencies within the CLB (Clearing & Brokerage) initiative.

When I first learned the project was being shelved, I was devastated. Six months of work—validated, tested, proven—sitting in a Figma file.

But here's what I learned about impact in large organizations:

Good Research Compounds

These research and design foundations are now informing the upcoming Wove New Account Opening (WoW NAO) initiative, which will rebuild and modernize the experience based on the tested concepts from this project.

  • The Documents Table design was adapted for another team's workflow redesign, reducing their NIGO rate by 22%
  • The Pre-Flow Setup pattern became a design principle across Wove—now applied to 4 other workflows

The Real Deliverable Was the Framework

This effort remains a key reference point for how Pershing approaches:

  • Guided workflows (the Rendezvous model)
  • Document orchestration (form status visibility)
  • Cross-team collaboration (shared progress views)

"Even when designs don't ship, good research and strategy always find their way back into the product."

🔮

Reflection: What I'd Do Differently

If I were to do this again—or tackle a similar problem in a startup environment—I would:

1. Front-Load the Documents Table Earlier in Discovery

We identified documents as a pain point, but didn't realize it was the critical driver of NIGO rates until validation. Had I quantified the document-related NIGO breakdown earlier (using support ticket data), I could have prioritized this solution from day one and potentially shipped it independently.

The Lesson

When you find the highest-leverage problem, isolate it and ship it first. Don't wait for the perfect end-to-end solution.

2. Push Harder for a Phased Rollout

The all-or-nothing approach made this project vulnerable to shifting priorities. If I had advocated for shipping the Documents Table + To-Do List as Phase 1 (the highest impact, lowest dependency features), we might have gotten something into users' hands sooner.

The Lesson

In large organizations, incremental value > perfect solutions. Ship the 20% that delivers 80% of the impact.

3. Quantify the Cost of Inaction

We had qualitative pain and validated outcomes, but I didn't translate the legacy system cost into dollar-value for executives. Had I led with "$1.2M annual savings for a mid-sized firm," the prioritization conversation might have been different.

The Lesson

Speak the language of the room. Designers speak in user pain; executives speak in ROI. Translate your insights into their currency.

4. Build Allies in Engineering Earlier

I focused heavily on user research and design validation, but didn't build strong relationships with engineering leads until late in the process. When platform dependencies became blockers, I didn't have the social capital to advocate effectively.

The Lesson

In complex org environments, your stakeholder map should include engineering leadership from day one. Technical feasibility is a design constraint, not an afterthought.

Final Thought

This case study represents how I approach product design: Start with the system-level problem, not the UI. Validate with data. Design frameworks, not just features. And when things don't ship, extract the learning and carry it forward.

The Rendezvous model, the Progress Orchestration framework, and the Form Orchestration system are now part of how I think about complex workflows. That's the real outcome.

This case study demonstrates end-to-end product thinking: from synthesizing messy research to designing concrete solutions to validating impact—even when external factors prevent immediate shipping.
